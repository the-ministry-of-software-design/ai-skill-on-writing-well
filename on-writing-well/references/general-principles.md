# General Principles — On Writing Well (Zinsser)

These are the principles you may directly apply when editing text. Work through each category methodically.

---

## 1. Eliminate Clutter

Clutter is the most common problem in writing. Look for and remove each of the following:

| Clutter type | Example → Fix |
|---|---|
| Redundant prepositions | "head up the project" → "head the project" |
| Duplicate meanings | "personal feeling" → "feeling"; "future plans" → "plans" |
| Inflated words | "utilize" → "use"; "assistance" → "help"; "commence" → "start"; "endeavour" → "try"; "obtain" → "get"; "in order to" → "to" |
| Euphemisms | "depressed socioeconomic area" → "slum"; "passed away" → "died" (where appropriate to context) |
| Qualifiers | remove "quite," "rather," "sort of," "a bit," "pretty much," "somewhat," "fairly," "very," "really" — these weaken statements |
| Filler phrases | "due to the fact that" → "because"; "at this point in time" → "now"; "in the event that" → "if"; "for the purpose of" → "to"; "it is important to note that" → (delete and state the point) |
| Redundant descriptions | "tall skyscraper"; "smile happily"; "past history"; "end result"; "unexpected surprise" |
| Throat-clearing openers | Sentences that start with "It is..." or "There is/are..." often delay the real point — restructure around the actual subject |

**Target: aim to cut unnecessary words wherever they appear. If a word or phrase is not doing real work, remove it.**

---

## 2. Active vs Passive Voice

- Prefer active voice: "Joe saw him" over "He was seen by Joe."
- Passive voice saps energy and distances the reader from the action.
- Flag passive constructions and rewrite them in active voice unless there is a specific reason to use passive (e.g., the subject is genuinely unknown or irrelevant).

---

## 3. Verbs

- Strong, active, specific verbs carry sentences forward.
- Replace weak verb + adverb combinations with a single stronger verb where possible.
  - "walked slowly" → "trudged" or "ambled"
  - "said loudly" → "announced" or "declared"
- Avoid noun-ified verbs (nominalisation): "make a decision" → "decide"; "provide assistance" → "help"; "give consideration to" → "consider."

---

## 4. Adjectives and Adverbs

- Remove adjectives and adverbs that are not doing essential, specific work.
- If an adjective simply repeats what the noun already implies, delete it.
- If an adverb simply restates what the verb already conveys, delete it.
- When an adjective IS kept, make sure it is precise and specific — not a vague intensifier.

---

## 5. Clichés

- Flag phrases that are tired, overused, or that arrive too easily.
- Common ones to watch for: "at the end of the day," "thinking outside the box," "hit the ground running," "it is what it is," "a perfect storm," "move the needle," "low-hanging fruit," "in today's fast-paced world," "each and every," "the bottom line is."
- Replace with plain language or a fresh, specific observation.

---

## 6. That vs Which

- Use **"that"** for restrictive clauses (no comma): "The book that I bought yesterday."
- Use **"which"** only for non-restrictive (parenthetical) clauses, preceded by a comma: "The book, which I bought yesterday, was excellent."
- If the clause is essential to meaning: "that." If it is additional information: "which."

---

## 7. Sentence Structure

- **Break long, overloaded sentences into shorter ones.** A sentence that runs past three clauses is usually better split.
- **Vary sentence length** to create rhythm. A short sentence after a run of longer ones provides emphasis and relief.
- Short sentences force clarity. When a sentence is hard to follow, it often means the thought isn't yet clear — break it apart.
- Avoid sentences that keep adding clauses with "and" or "but" when a full stop would be stronger.

---

## 8. Paragraph Structure

- Keep paragraphs short — they provide visual breathing room and signal discrete units of thought.
- Each paragraph should have one clear purpose.
- The opening of each paragraph should connect logically to the close of the previous one. Look for abrupt transitions and smooth them.

---

## 9. Punctuation

- **Dashes (—)** are valuable for amplification or parenthetical thoughts where a comma would feel too light and parentheses too heavy.
- **"But" as a sentence opener** is perfectly acceptable and creates useful emphasis. Do not avoid it.
- **Contractions** add warmth and naturalism — "it's," "don't," "we'll." Avoid avoiding them out of false formality.
- **Exclamation points** — remove them unless genuinely essential. Understatement is more effective. One exclamation point in a piece is one too many unless the moment truly demands it.

---

## 10. Hedging and Confidence

- Remove hedging language that weakens assertions: "surprisingly," "of course," "needless to say," "obviously," "it seems," "arguably," "perhaps" (unless genuine uncertainty is being expressed).
- Let the material speak for itself. Trust the reader.
- Opinions should be expressed firmly. If the writer is taking a position, the language should reflect that.

---

## 11. Openings (The Lead)

- The first sentence must earn its place — it should pull the reader to the second sentence.
- Flag opening sentences that begin with vague scene-setting, excessive context-laying, or biographical throat-clearing.
- If the real story starts on paragraph two or three, that is often where the piece should actually begin.

---

## 12. Endings

- The piece should end when it naturally concludes — not with a trailing summary, and not with an extra thought tacked on.
- Flag endings that summarise what has already been said.
- The ideal ending either brings the narrative full circle, echoes the opening, or closes on a meaningful, resonant note.
- If the last paragraph can be deleted without loss, delete it.

---

## 13. Unity

- Check for consistency in point of view (first person vs third person) — it should not shift mid-piece without reason.
- Check for consistency in verb tense — unintentional tense shifts should be corrected.
- Check for consistency in tone — a sudden shift from formal to casual, or casual to academic, is usually unintentional and should be flagged.

---

## 14. Spelling

- Correct all spelling errors. Include every spelling correction in the summary of changes.
