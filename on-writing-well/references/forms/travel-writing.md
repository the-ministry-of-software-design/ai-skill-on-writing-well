# Form-Specific Tips: Travel Writing

Apply these in addition to the general principles when the text is identified as travel writing.

---

## What to look for and apply

**Avoid well-worn travel clichés**
- Flag phrases like "hidden gem," "off the beaten path," "bustling streets," "picturesque," "charming," "vibrant," "breathtaking," "locals going about their daily lives," "steeped in history," "a feast for the senses."
- Replace with specific, original description. What exactly did it look like, sound like, smell like? A concrete sensory detail beats any cliché.

**Choose words with surprise, strength, and precision**
- Vague descriptors ("beautiful," "amazing," "wonderful") should be replaced with specific observations.
- Ask: what is *particular* about this place or moment? What would a reader not expect?

**Human activity brings locales alive**
- Flag passages that describe only scenery, buildings, or geography without people.
- If people are absent, note this as a suggestion: the piece would be strengthened by including specific human encounters, conversations, or characters observed in the location.

**Find the central idea driving the piece**
- Travel writing should be organised around a central insight or personal relationship to the place — not simply a chronological itinerary.
- If the piece reads as a list of events or places visited without a unifying angle, flag this for the writer.

**Be selective — quality over quantity**
- If the piece covers many places, days, or attractions, look for sections that feel thin, rushed, or list-like and flag them as candidates for cutting or deepening.
- One well-described scene beats three briefly mentioned ones.

**Perspective and voice**
- The writer's personal relationship to the place is part of the story. If the piece reads as purely descriptive with no personal angle, flag this: what did this place mean to the writer? What brought them there? What changed?
