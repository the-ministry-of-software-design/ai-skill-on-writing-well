# Form-Specific Tips: Reviews and Criticism

Apply these in addition to the general principles when the text is identified as a review or piece of criticism (book review, film review, restaurant review, product review, cultural criticism, etc.).

---

## What to look for and apply

**Write about work you like or love**
- This is primarily a tip for the writer (see user-only-tips.md), but editorially: flag reviews that seem reluctant or where the writer's enthusiasm is absent. A review without a genuine perspective is weak.
- Reviews of things the writer doesn't care about tend to read as obligation — the prose becomes flat. If this is evident in the text, note it.

**Build credibility through clarity before attempting humor**
- Flag attempts at wit or humor that appear before the writer has established their authority on the subject.
- The reader needs to trust the reviewer's judgment before they can enjoy the reviewer's personality.

**Support opinions with evidence**
- Flag evaluative claims ("this is the best," "it's deeply moving," "the pacing is poor") that are not supported by specific examples from the work under review.
- Each strong claim should be grounded in a specific moment, quote, scene, or detail from the work.

**Avoid plot summary as a substitute for analysis**
- Flag extended passages that describe what happens in a work (plot, plot, plot) without interpretation or evaluation.
- Plot summary should be kept to the minimum needed for the reader to follow the argument.

**Take a clear position**
- A review that hedges ("it has strengths and weaknesses," "some readers will love it, some won't") is not doing its job.
- Flag excessively balanced conclusions and suggest the writer commit to a position.

**The opening**
- A strong review opens with a specific observation, scene, or claim — not with background on the author/director/subject.
- Flag review openings that begin with biography, history, or context before the writer has hooked the reader.
