# Form-Specific Tips: Science and Technical Writing

Apply these in addition to the general principles when the text is identified as science or technical writing (explainers, articles, reports, documentation, blog posts about technical subjects).

---

## What to look for and apply

**Write for a reader who knows nothing about the topic**
- Flag passages that assume knowledge the reader may not have — unexplained jargon, acronyms on first use, concepts introduced without context.
- Every technical term used for the first time should be defined in plain language, or replaced with the plain language equivalent.

**The inverted pyramid structure**
Technical writing should proceed from most-to-least critical:
1. The one fact the reader absolutely must know — this comes first.
2. Each subsequent sentence or paragraph broadens understanding of that fact.
3. Progress from concrete, specific details toward broader significance and implications.

If the piece buries its key finding or conclusion, flag this and suggest moving it to the opening.

**Sequential storytelling — no fanciful leaps**
- Each step in an explanation must follow logically from the last.
- Flag any moments where the writer seems to assume a connection the reader will not be able to follow on their own.
- Flag implied truths — conclusions presented as obvious that haven't been established.

**Write as a person, not in academic style**
- Remove academic hedging phrases: "it has been observed that," "it may be noted that," "it is widely believed that."
- Remove unnecessary passive constructions that academic writing favours: "the experiment was conducted" → "we conducted the experiment" or "researchers conducted the experiment."
- Remove noun-heavy, abstract language. Replace with active, concrete equivalents.
- The writer should explain the process as if walking someone through it in conversation — this is both clearer and reveals gaps in the writer's own understanding.

**Make abstractions concrete**
- Flag abstract claims without concrete examples or illustrations.
- Suggest that the writer ground each key concept in a specific example, analogy, or comparison a non-specialist reader can hold onto.
