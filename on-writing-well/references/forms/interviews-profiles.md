# Form-Specific Tips: Interviews and Profiles

Apply these in addition to the general principles when the text is identified as an interview, profile, or biographical piece.

---

## What to look for and apply

**Quotations**
- Direct quotes should be preserved carefully and not paraphrased into blandness. If a quote is vivid and specific, it should stay as a quote.
- Very long blocks of uninterrupted quotation can lose the reader — flag them and suggest breaking with authorial context or interpretation.
- Weak quotes (where the quoted person says something generic or obvious) may be better rendered as paraphrase. Flag these.

**Authorial presence**
- The writer's voice and perspective should frame the quotations, not disappear between them.
- Flag passages where the piece reads as a sequence of quotes with very little authorial connective tissue. The writer should be guiding the reader through the material.

**Showing vs telling**
- A profile that simply tells us a person is interesting, admirable, or impressive is weaker than one that shows us through specific detail and direct quotation.
- Flag evaluative claims about the subject ("he is a visionary," "she is deeply committed") that are not demonstrated by evidence in the piece.

**Specific, distinctive detail**
- The most memorable profiles are built on specific, unexpected details — the subject's habits, environment, turns of phrase, contradictions.
- Flag generic descriptions of the subject (their job title, generic personality descriptors) and note that specific, observed detail would be stronger.

**Structure**
- Profiles often work best when organised around a central insight or tension about the subject, not chronologically from birth to present.
- If the piece feels like a Wikipedia entry (sequential biography), flag this and suggest finding the central human tension or insight to organise around.
