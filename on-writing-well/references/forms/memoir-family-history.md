# Form-Specific Tips: Memoir and Family History

Apply these in addition to the general principles when the text is identified as memoir, personal essay, or family history writing.

---

## What to look for and apply

**Human elements — readers identify with people, not abstractions**
- Flag passages where events or facts are recounted without human feeling, reaction, or consequence.
- Dates, genealogical data, and historical context matter — but they should be woven around the human beings involved, not listed abstractly.

**Specific, unusual, distinctive details**
- Generic descriptions ("she was a kind woman," "he worked hard") should be flagged. Specific remembered details are more powerful: what she wore, what she said, what she smelled like, what she always did on Sundays.
- Look for moments of specificity in the text and affirm them — these are the strongest parts.
- Flag passages where the writer has described an emotion without grounding it in a specific moment or detail.

**Be selective**
- Memoir does not need to be complete or chronological. The strongest pieces select a few telling moments and go deep.
- If the piece feels like a comprehensive timeline, flag it and suggest: what are the two or three moments that actually matter? Start there.

**Show the writer in the scene**
- Flag passages where the writer reports events at a distance, without their own presence, reaction, or interiority.
- The writer's own confusion, conflict, love, grief — these are what make memoir distinct from reportage.

**The universal in the particular**
- Memoir should reach beyond the personal to something readers can recognise in their own lives.
- If the piece feels too narrowly private, flag this: what is the broader human truth this story illustrates? That doesn't need to be stated explicitly — but it should be findable in the details and framing.
