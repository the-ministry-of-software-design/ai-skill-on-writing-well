# Tips Only the Writer Can Apply

These are principles from *On Writing Well* that cannot be acted on by an editor — they require the writer's own judgment, self-knowledge, and revision. Present these to the user as a list of considerations after returning the revised text.

---

**Voice and authenticity**
- Write as yourself, not as an imagined "writer." Your personality is your commodity. Don't adopt false formality or try to sound impressive — your natural voice is your greatest asset.
- There is no style store. Style is organic — as much a part of you as your hair. Relax and have confidence that your way of seeing things is enough.
- Write like you talk. Use the language and rhythm of your actual speech. If you would never say a word aloud, don't put it on the page.

**Purpose and focus**
- Decide the single main point you want readers to retain. Write it down before you revise. Then check: does every paragraph serve that one point?
- Find your corner of the subject. You don't have to cover everything — bite off one provocative, specific angle and pursue it fully.
- Only write about subjects that genuinely interest you. Your enthusiasm (or lack of it) shows in the writing. If you are bored, the reader will be too.

**Revision habits**
- Read your work aloud. This is the single most revealing revision tool. Rhythm problems, awkward phrasing, tonal inconsistencies, and run-on sentences all become immediately audible in ways they aren't on the page.
- Give yourself breaks between revision passes. Your subconscious continues working on problems when you rest. A piece read after a day away looks different than a piece read the moment you put it down.

**The lead (opening)**
- Ask yourself: where does the real story actually begin? Often the strongest opening is buried in paragraph two or three. The material you wrote first was you warming up.

**Confidence**
- Express opinions firmly and take clear stands. Readers want a guide with a point of view, not someone who hedges everything. If you believe something, say it plainly.
- Develop one recognisable voice across your writing. Readers who return to a writer do so partly because they know what they're getting. Consistency in how you see the world and how you express it builds that trust.
